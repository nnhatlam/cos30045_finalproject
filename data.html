Chapter 1

Are We Funding Campaigns for the Wrong Drivers?

Picture a distracted driver. For most of us, the stereotype is a young student scrolling at a red light. The data tells another story: drivers aged 26-39 and 40-64 dominate fine counts, while under-17 drivers represent the smallest share.

Insight: The 26–39 and 40–64 age groups together account for approximately 78% of all fines — highlighting that mid-life working-age Australians are the most frequent offenders, not teenagers.

Chapter 2

When Does a Distraction Become a Criminal Record?

Most offences end with fines and demerit points, but legal outcomes can escalate. Drivers aged 26-39 face the highest levels of both charges and arrests, raising questions about repeat offending, co-occurring violations, and enforcement context.

Arrests by Age Group

Charges by Age Group

Insight: Charges outnumber arrests by ~40× to 1. The 26–39 group faces the highest volume in both categories, reinforcing their status as the most heavily enforced cohort.

Chapter 3

Is AI Surveillance Replacing Highway Patrols?

National enforcement infrastructure is uneven. In NSW and ACT, AI fixed cameras generate the overwhelming majority of detections. In NT, SA, and WA, enforcement still depends heavily on police detection.

Camera vs. Police Fines by Jurisdiction

Jurisdictions ordered by total fine volume

Policy note: ACT has the highest camera:police ratio — 142:1. NT records zero camera fines, demonstrating significant infrastructure inequality across jurisdictions.

Chapter 4

Does Your Postcode Alter Who Gets Caught?

Although total fine volumes differ significantly by state, age composition is remarkably stable. Across jurisdictions, 26-39 and 40-64 consistently account for the largest shares, suggesting nationally consistent behavioural risk among working-age drivers.

Chapter 5

Is Roadside Justice Equal Across State Lines?

Fair enforcement should mean similar outcomes for similar behaviour, regardless of location. Yet the data reveals stark inequality: while some states resolve almost all incidents with standard fines, the Northern Territory records a much higher rate of criminal charges and severe outcomes.

Key finding: NT's charge rate of 23.96% is 8× that of WA (17.64%) and vastly higher than eastern states at 0%. This warrants further investigation into enforcement practices and reporting consistency.

Chapter 6

The National Footprint

By visualising the proportion of the nation's total mobile phone driving fines via a single web polygon, we can instantly spot the massive imbalance in enforcement across the country, showing which jurisdictions issue the most and the least fines overall.

Insight: NSW utterly dominates the footprint (nearly 45%). The radar employs a square root scale to prevent smaller states like TAS & NT from becoming invisible dots, highlighting the sheer volume generated by fixed AI networks.
